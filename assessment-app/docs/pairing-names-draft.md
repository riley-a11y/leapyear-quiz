## Notes for Riley

**Pairing names are always read as: "The [Name]" after the identity statement.** For example: "You are a Builder with strong Philosopher tendencies. You are *The Architect.*"

**Selection criteria to consider:**
- Does it feel like something a 17-year-old would share? ("I got The Seeker" vs. "I got The Ordered Mind")
- Is it evocative without being limiting? (Avoid names that sound like job titles)
- Does it capture the *tension* between the two types, not just the primary?
- Could you imagine a student saying "that's SO me" when they see it?

**Names I'm most confident about:** The Architect, The Seeker, The Artisan, The Pioneer, The Sage, The Free Spirit, The Curator, The Playmaker, The Powerhouse.

**Names that might need rethinking:** The Execution Machine (too corporate), The Beautiful Problem (too abstract), The Calculated Mover (too cold). Flagged for your review.

**Total pairings covered:** 20 (16 cross-orientation + 4 same-orientation) = all possible non-same-type pairings where the two types come from different orientation categories, plus all 4 within-orientation pairings. The remaining 36 pairings (same-type primary/secondary is impossible, and some cross-pairings are covered by reversing primary/secondary) can reuse these names with the primary archetype emphasized.

**On reversals:** Builder-Philosopher and Philosopher-Builder are different people. The pairing name stays the same, but the output narrative leads with a different primary. "The Architect" who leads with Builder energy feels different from "The Architect" who leads with Philosopher energy — and that's handled by the primary narrative, not the name.
