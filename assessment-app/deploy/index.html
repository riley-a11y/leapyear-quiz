Everyone wants to know what you're going to do next. We think there's a better place to start.

At 18, the world narrows. Suddenly every conversation is the same question wearing different clothes. What's your major? Where are you applying? What's the plan?

Those questions matter. But they're limiting — and if you're not careful, they'll put you in a box before you've had a chance to explore what's actually inside you. You don't need to feel pressure to make a decision that will consume the next three or four years of your life before you've had the chance to discover answers to some much bigger questions first.

Questions like: Who are you? What do you love? What are you great at? What does the world need from someone like you? And the one question we hope you'll carry for the rest of your life: who are you becoming?

You are coming of age at an inflection point. The challenges facing your generation are real and unprecedented — broken education models that feel more like factories than formation, a leadership vacuum in nearly every institution that matters, an attention economy that feeds off your focus and gives back nothing of substance, and an explosion of artificial intelligence that is reshaping the world faster than anyone can keep up with.

But this moment is not just defined by its problems. It's also defined by its opportunity. There has never been a greater need for people who know who they are. People who have invested in becoming equipped. People who can lead with character and bring genuine creativity and humanness to a world that feels increasingly artificial.

Your generation has the chance to be the one that brings color back — that refuses to accept the gray, efficient, machine-driven version of life and instead builds something beautiful in its place.

We think of it as a new Renaissance. And every Renaissance needs people who come alive in different ways.

Some people come alive when they're understanding deep truths about the human condition. Others come alive when they're rallying people around a vision bigger than all of them. Some come alive when they're making something from nothing — the craft of turning an idea into something real.

Others come alive in the presence of beauty, or in the thrill of a place they've never been, or in the quiet moment when someone across the table finally feels known.

The way you come alive is specific to you. It's not generic. It's not the same as the person sitting next to you. There is a particular way that you engage the world — a drive, an orientation, a posture toward reality — that is as unique as your fingerprint and as central as your heartbeat.
