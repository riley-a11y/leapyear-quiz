# LeapYear Personality Assessment — Project Context

## What This Is

A personality assessment positioned as a lead magnet for LeapYear (startleapyear.com), a 12-month gap year program in Austin, Texas for high school graduates. The assessment is a first step in vocational wayfinding — helping students take one small step toward discovering how they come alive. It lives on the LeapYear domain but is its own distinct experience, visually connected to but separate from the main LeapYear site.

## The Assessment Mechanics

- 85 questions drawn from well-researched personality and interest instruments
- Takes ~13 minutes
- Produces a profile with a **primary archetype** (core type) and **secondary archetype** that creates tension and richness
- 8 archetypes total, organized into 4 orientations
- Also measures growth mindset (fixed/mixed/growth)
- Free, with a coaching call CTA at the end

## The 8 Archetypes

Organized into 4 orientations:

**Thinking:**
- Philosopher — "You ask the questions others are afraid to sit with."
- Analyst — "You see the systems underneath and know how to shift them."

**Doing:**
- Builder — "You take what's imagined and make it real."
- Organizer — "You see the structure that should exist and build it."

**Relating:**
- Connector — "People feel known in your presence."
- Mobilizer — "You rally people around something bigger than all of them."

**Experiencing:**
- Creator — "You make beauty from nothing."
- Explorer — "The unknown is where you feel most alive."

## The World-Building

### The Cultural Moment
Students face a convergence of unprecedented challenges: broken education models (factory-style credentialism), a leadership vacuum across institutions, an extractive attention economy, and AI disruption that displaces something essentially human. Students feel like "we've lost the plot." But this moment is also ripe with opportunity — there has never been a greater need for people who know who they are, are equipped, can lead with character, and bring genuine creativity and humanness to a world that feels increasingly artificial.

### The Redemptive Generation
A generational narrative that the assessment lives inside. This generation is being called to restore, renew, and revive what is lost and beautiful about older times — with a modern expression. The word "redemptive" means buying back, restoring, making whole. They are not defined by the labels the world has given them (lazy, anxious, fragile, screen-addicted) but by who they actually are and who they are becoming.

### The New Renaissance
The current moment framed as the beginning of a new Renaissance — an explosion of the human after a period where technology, efficiency, and the machine have dominated. Like the historical Renaissance, this calls for people who refuse to be reduced to one function. We need all different kinds of people coming alive in their specific way, but every person needs to develop the full range of their humanity. The 8 archetypes map to this — different ways of bringing color back to a gray world.

### The Enemy
Stated plainly, not metaphorically. There is an enemy whose strategy is to label an entire generation with an identity that isn't theirs. If the generation can be bowed into submission — given labels it lives into — those labels become a self-fulfilling destruction. The assessment pushes back: you are not your symptoms. There is something underneath — a design, a drive — and it is good.

### Devices vs. Instruments
Technology itself isn't the enemy. A device uses you (captures attention, extracts time). An instrument extends you (amplifies capacity, serves purpose). The assessment tries to get beneath surface-level behavior to the animating drive that would be there even without a screen.

### Theological Grounding (Implicit, Not Explicit)
- Humans are "strength, mind, soul, complex designed for love" (Andy Crouch)
- Life fully alive = being animated by the Spirit of God (not achievement, experience, or purpose)
- The Garden Mandate (create, cultivate, multiply, bring into order) maps suggestively to the 4 orientations
- Each orientation potentially reflects a facet of the image of God
- The Gospel frame: the Kingdom of God is at hand — available, breaking in now
- Theology shows up as breadcrumbs, not billboards. The assessment should be valuable and resonant for students who don't share the Christian framework, while students who do should sense depth underneath

### The Call
"Be who you are. Be who you are made to be." The assessment result functions as a commissioning — a sending, not just a label. It says: here's who you are, here's what you carry, here's what the mission needs from you, here's where you'll be attacked, here's what's true about you underneath all of it.

### The Emotional Target
After reading their result, students should feel:
- **Seen** — "someone finally understood something about me"
- **Located** — "there's a story happening and I have a place in it"
- **Honest about their shadow** — "that part stung a little, but it's true"
- **Curious about the gap** — "I can feel there's more"
- **Invited, not sold** — "they understand what I need"

## Language Guidelines

**Use:** "come alive," "animating drive," "design," "steward," "formation," "equipped," "the story you're in," "tension," "shadow," "growth edge," "bring color," "Renaissance," "becoming"

**Avoid:** "passion," "purpose," "gifting," "calling" (save for program), "personality type," "broken" (for the student — systems are broken, student is not), "potential" (use "design"), "find your purpose" (too much pressure), AI-sounding constructions, corporate jargon

**Tone:** Like someone who knows you talking to you. Second person. Prose paragraphs, not bullets. Empathy before challenge. Warm but direct. Honest about limitations. Conviction at the close. The voice should feel conversational with authority — specific rather than abstract, short sentences after long ones, fragments that land.

## The Site Structure

### Landing Page
- Introduces the assessment
- Positions the cultural moment and Renaissance frame (briefly)
- Shows the 8 archetypes as a teaser (curiosity bait — "which one am I?")
- CTA to start the assessment
- Should be visually compelling and not text-heavy

### Results Page
- **The Card** — A shareable archetype card is the first thing you see after the quiz. It appears after a loading animation. This is the screenshot/Instagram story moment. Each archetype has its own color, icon/illustration, and identity statement. Portrait-oriented, bold, distinct per type.
- Below the card: full profile content including core type description, secondary type description, the tension between them, shadow cards, archetype scores (radar chart + score bars), growth mindset gauge, and a commissioning CTA with coaching call booking.

### Sample Output (Organizer/Explorer pairing)
The example output we've been working with is for an Organizer (core) / Explorer (secondary) pairing. The tension is "Structure vs. Freedom." There are 4 shadow cards. Growth mindset is "Mixed." Scores: Organizer 70, Explorer 64, Creator 55, Analyst 53, Connector 51, Mobilizer 50, Builder 50, Philosopher 50.

## Brand Relationship

The assessment site is **connected to but distinct from** the main LeapYear site. It should feel like walking into a different room in the same house. The LeapYear brand system (documented in the leapyear-brand skill) provides the foundation — Neulis Neue and Area Normal fonts, the warm/grounded/nature-rooted personality — but the assessment site extends it with its own expanded color palette for the 8 archetypes and its own visual atmosphere.

Key LeapYear brand elements to maintain connection:
- Fonts: Neulis Neue (display), Area Normal (body) via Typekit kit `gxr6ytx`
- Logo assets from LeapYear CDN
- Warm, never cold. Grounded, never flashy. Aspirational, not preachy.
- No pure black or pure white backgrounds
- Flame (#DD5E32) as the LeapYear accent, but the assessment site has its own color world

## Design Exploration So Far

### Direction 1: "Field Guide + Studio" (Built — saved in `direction-1-field-guide/`)
The direction we've built and refined. Key characteristics:
- Warm cream/parchment base palette (`#FAF7F1`, `#F0EBE1`)
- Serif/sans typography mixing — added Instrument Serif (italic) as a third typeface alongside Neulis Neue and Area Normal. Headlines mix bold sans with flowing italic serif ("How do you *come alive?*")
- Paper grain texture overlay (subtle SVG noise filter)
- Archetype cards as the centerpiece — 3:4 portrait ratio, each archetype's color as background, noise texture for materiality, SVG line-art illustration per type, serif italic identity line, category tag (Thinking/Doing/Relating/Experiencing), roman numeral numbering
- Swipeable carousel with fade edges, dot indicators, drag-to-scroll
- Cards tilt on hover (~-0.8deg rotation + lift)
- Results page: loading animation → card reveal (enters with scale + rotation), drawing radar chart (polygon traces itself via stroke-dashoffset), animated score bars with counting numbers, animated spectrum/gauge dots, pull quotes in serif italic with colored left border, shadow cards with animated border reveal on hover
- Parchment section backgrounds alternating with cream
- Roman numerals for numbering throughout
- Minimal copy, visual-first
- Dark green (#17322D) used only for the commissioning section at the end

### Other Directions Explored (Concepts Only)

**"The Cartography"** — Celestial maps meets wayfinding meets vintage atlas. Star charts, constellation lines, old cartographic lettering mixed with modern type. Muted jewel-tone palette against deep navy and warm parchment. The radar chart becomes a literal star map. Subtle parallax star-field. The wayfinding metaphor made visual. Tone: wonder and weight.

**"The Studio"** — Mixed media, collage, bold color blocks. Torn paper edges, watercolor washes, paint-swatch chips. Matisse cut-outs meets Virgil Abloh meets indie zine culture. Each archetype gets its own color AND texture (charcoal, watercolor, ink, graph paper). The most overtly Gen Z direction. Sticker-like elements that feel collectible. Screenshot-optimized. Tone: confident, playful but not juvenile.

### Key Design Decisions Made
1. The card is the product — it's the shareable moment, the thing people screenshot and post
2. Each archetype needs a genuinely distinct visual identity (color + illustration + energy)
3. Mobile-first — the primary audience (17-18 year olds) engages on phones
4. The landing page should tease all 8 cards to create "which one am I?" curiosity
5. Less copy, more visual. The original landing page copy was too text-heavy.
6. The results page should lead with the card reveal, then unfold the detailed profile below
7. Interactive elements that work well: drawing radar chart, animated counting score numbers, sliding spectrum dots, card reveal animation with rotation
8. The site needs real illustrations/icons for each archetype — not just Unicode symbols, but not hand-drawn SVG attempts either (those looked amateur). This is an open problem that likely needs actual designed assets.
